# Critter Cafe: the free nutrition game my 9-year-old and I designed together

When my son Adam was born just over nine years ago, he had digestion issues. We had many sleepless nights, and eating problems that took years to resolve. On the plus side, Adam became very aware of what he eats and what works for him. This year he stood up in front of his class and presented the science of food. This educational game was conceived together as a natural extension of that presentation — and it is free for all kids and parents to use for teaching purposes.

**Play it here: [daridor9.github.io/critter-cafe](https://daridor9.github.io/critter-cafe/)** — no ads, no accounts, no downloads. It runs in any browser.

## The idea: make the kid the expert

Most nutrition education talks *at* children. Eat your vegetables. Sugar is bad. Five a day. Kids nod, and nothing sticks.

Critter Cafe flips the chair. In the game, *you* are the family nutritionist. Your family — a baby, a child, an adult, and a grandparent — comes to *you*. The baby can't have honey. The grandparent shouldn't have another coffee. The child is starving after school. Nobody lectures the player, because the player is the one who knows things. Every bit of nutrition knowledge in the game arrives the way knowledge actually sticks: because you needed it to solve a problem.

That framing came directly from watching Adam. A kid who spent years figuring out what his own body could handle doesn't want to be taught about food. He wants to be consulted.

## Three things every meal costs

Every food in the game has a price in coins, a preparation time in minutes, and real nutritional content — calories, protein, carbs, and fat, using realistic rounded values. Every meal has a budget of money and time.

And here is the whole game in one sentence: **cheap, fast, healthy — pick two.**

Eggs for everyone? You're out of time before school. Salmon for everyone? You've spent the whole dinner budget on one ingredient. The cheapest plan busts the clock; the fastest plan shortchanges someone's body. Finding the mix — hearty protein for the working adult, pasta for the hungry kid, something gentle for grandma, something mashable for the baby — is exactly the puzzle every family solves every single day. Children who play it start to understand why dinner is the way it is.

## Different bodies, different needs

Each family member has a daily calorie target, a metabolism rate, and activity costs — the baby needs about 700 calories a day, the child 1,800, the adult 2,200. Serve a meal and each character reacts in a speech bubble: green for a great fit, yellow for okay, red for genuinely unsafe.

The red reactions are my favorite teaching moments, because they're all true:

- Give honey to the baby and she'll tell you honey is dangerous for babies (it is — infant botulism).
- Give coffee to the child: "Coffee isn't for kids!"
- In the East Asian kitchen, raw fish is unsafe for the baby, and mochi is a choking risk for the grandparent — a real public-health warning in Japan.
- In the Latin American kitchen, kids discover that beans and rice together make a complete protein, and that avocado is a famously perfect first food for babies.

At the end of each day, the game shows the honest arithmetic: what each person ate, what their body burned, what they actually needed. The first time you play, almost everyone goes to bed hungry — and that's the deepest lesson in the game. A body is not fueled by one good meal. It's fueled by a steady stream of decent ones.

One rule we set on day one and never broke: **no body-shaming, ever.** Consequences in the game are about energy, mood, and growth — never appearance. A poorly fed character is tired, not mocked.

## Every culture solved nutrition

The game ships with three switchable kitchens — Mediterranean, East Asian, and Latin American — each with its own real pantry, its own price-and-time tradeoffs, and its own teaching moments. There is no "correct" cuisine in the game, because there is no correct cuisine in the world. Every food culture on Earth solved the same problem with different ingredients. A kid in Tel Aviv, Tokyo, or Mexico City should be able to open this game and find food that looks like home.

## What's in the game today

You plan a full day — breakfast, packed school lunches, lunch at home, a snack, and dinner — building combination plates from about twenty foods per kitchen. You can add family members (more kids? grandparents? up to eight), name them, adjust the budgets to make the puzzle easier or harder, and play day after day. Everything saves automatically in your browser.

## How it was built

Adam designed; I built — together with Claude, Anthropic's AI assistant, over a string of evenings. Adam made the calls a designer makes: that players should hear characters *speak* rather than read stat bars, that the child should be able to eat lunch at home some days, that budgets needed to be bigger and adjustable. I turned the dials. The entire thing is open source.

It's also the sister project of [Critter Forge](https://daridor9.github.io/critter-forge/), the biophysics creature-design game we built first — and one day the two will connect, when the creatures you design start needing meal plans for their journeys. But that's a story for another post.

## Free, for any kid, any classroom

Critter Cafe is free and always will be. No ads, no tracking, no sign-ups. If you're a parent, play it with your kid and let *them* be the expert at the table for once. If you're a teacher, it runs on anything with a browser, and a full day takes about ten minutes to play.
